Easy price comparison avoids:

Price Comparison Prevention

  • This pattern involves making it difficult to compare the prices of products.
  • Price comparison prevention is problematic because allowing the prices of products to be easily comparable on a shopping website helps users make the best decision for purchases.
  • When this pattern is practiced, users that have selected a lower quality item will feel more obligated to purchase it due to how time-consuming it would be to investigate further on whether or not they are truly getting the best deal.
  • An example: the screenshot provided demonstrates price comparison prevention in the form of forcing the user to add their item to the cart before showing them the product's price.
  • Another way this pattern manifests itself involves bundling products together to make it harder to determine the unit price.
  • The best way to avoid this deceptive pattern is to only use one unit when listing the prices of products on your website, and making these prices easily visible.

Making ads on a site obvious avoids:

Disguised Ads

  • Disguised ads are advertisements that trick the user into clicking on them, by being designed to look like another type of content on the website.
  • An example: in the provided screenshot, there initially appears to be two customer reviews underneath the "See all photos" button, but the little yellow icons in the upper left (That the blue arrow is pointing to) reveals these "reviews" are in fact advertisements.

Showing users alternate options before the option they want is:

Misdirection

  • This pattern occurs when designers want to take the user's attention off certain elements of a page by making other elements more noticeable in comparison.
  • An example: the provided screenshot shows the webpage the user is directed to when attempting to cancel their Amazon Prime membership. The "End Membership" button is located at the bottom of the webpage, with three alternative options being placed on top of this button that the user must scroll past.

Charging for things in between steps of the checkout process is:

Sneaking into Basket

  • This pattern is typically used to sell extra products or services to a user.
  • Can be done by either automatically adding the extra products to the user's cart at checkout, or by including a checkbox opting in for the product that is automatically left checked.
  • The user can be further manipulated into buying this extra product if the process to remove it from their purchases is too time-consuming (Also see roach motel).
  • An example: in the provided screenshot, an extra magazine and mug were added to this user's purchases and cannot be removed unless the user returns to their bag.
  • To avoid using this pattern but still accomplish its main goal, designers can instead ask the user whether or not they'd like to buy the extra item instead of sneakily forcing them to buy it.
    • Additionally, if the user ignores the offer, the website should not automatically add the item to the user's purchases.

Engaging in privacy zuckering:

Privacy Zuckering

  • Privacy zuckering tricks users into unknowingly sharing more personal information about themselves than they are comfortable with.
  • The name of this pattern references how FaceBook's privacy settings were originally hard to control, thus causing users to give away too much of their personal information.
  • Presently, this pattern often occurs in the form of the terms and conditions of an online service.
  • An example: the above screenshot shows the terms and privacy policy for Whatsapp. Page one of the terms and conditions does not mention FaceBook and also gives the user the option to agree to the terms without reading them in their entirety. If the user were to agree without reading the next page, they would be unaware that by agreeing to the privacy policy, their account data will be shared with FaceBook to improve the content of the ads they will see when using the service.
  • While using personal data to improve advertising may not seem harmful, it can potentially lead to effects such as being denied services like insurance.
  • To avoid privacy zuckering:
    • Make the wording in terms and conditions as concise and easy to understand as possible.
    • Make the terms and conditions themselves easy to locate and access.
    • Users should be encouraged to read through all of the terms, either through the wording or by making them unable to move on to the next page of the site without reading through the terms and conditions.
    • These suggestions are not infallible - making it required to read all the terms and conditions, in particular, will likely lead to the user losing interest in the website - but they do allow for users that do read them to be more likely to finish and understand who their personal data will be shared with.

Asking users straightforward questions avoids:

Trick Questions

  • Trick questions take advantage of users who read quickly.
  • This pattern words questions so they appear to ask one thing when they are in fact asking another, thus tricking users into giving a desired response.
  • Trick questions can use double negatives, or take advantage of certain expectations users have when answering questions.
  • An example: the above screenshot asks a trick question regarding communication preferences by listing the negative response above the positive response, whereas we are used to seeing the positive response be listed first. Thus, a user reading quickly may accidentally consent to sharing their personal data when they didn't want to.

Pressuring users to not opt out of something is:

Confirmshaming

  • Confirmshaming occurs when giving a user the choice between opting in or out of something, and the designer guilt-trips the user into opting in through their wording.
  • An example: in the pop-up above, the option to decline the discount the pop-up offers is worded to sound less appealing, thus pressuring the user into accepting the discount, even if it is unwillingly.
  • To avoid confirmshaming:
    • Prioritize being direct when wording options.
    • Test the site with users for feedback.
    • Test the site on yourself by reading your options out loud and asking yourself whether or not they sound manipulative.

Making opting in for a service easier than opting out creates:

Roach Motels

  • Roach motels make it easy to begin using a service, but makes the opt out process harder and longer.
  • This pattern is used to pressure users to continue using a service even when they do want to opt out of it.
  • An example: when purchasing tickets from Livenation, the website will automatically subscribe the user to Rolling Stone magazine during the checkout process unless the user unchecks a box (See also: Sneak into basket). If the user does unwillingly become subscribed to the magazine, the opt out process, as screenshotted above, is much more time-consuming, requiring the user to either visit Rolling Stone's website or contact customer service to cancel subscription, and to fill out a form and mail it if they want a rebate.
  • Other deceptive patterns can be used to create roach motels as well, such as:
    • Wording the option to opt out to sound less appealing (See also: confirmshaming).
    • Designing the opt out button to be harder to find on the page (See also: misdirection).
  • While it is normal for designers to want users to continue using their service, they nonetheless should not resort to roach motels to achieve this goal. Hence, opt out processes should be simplified as much as possible.

Notifying users of the end of a free trial avoids:

Forced Continuity

  • This pattern applies to services that offer a free trial period for the user and, upon the end of the free trial, automatically begins charging the user for the paid version of the service without informing them.
  • Upgraded forced continuity adds to this by making it hard for the user to opt out of the automatic renewal.
  • An example: the provided screenshot shows the sign-up for a free trial of Audible, with the text at the bottom revealing that the user will start being charged each month upon the trial ending. Although the user can cancel the trial at any time, if they're not given a reminder that their trial is ending soon, this would still be forced continuity, as Audible would then be taking advantage of the possibility of the user forgetting about their free trial during the thirty day period to make money off of them.
  • To avoid forced continuity, always send users a notification when their free trial for a service is close to ending so they can cancel their subscription without being charged.

Sending users' friends invitation messages via users' email is:

Friend Spamming

  • This pattern is the process of spamming a userâ€™s contacts with emails claiming to be from the user themselves.
  • Spamming a user's contacts under the user's name can negatively impact their relationships with those contacts, and is also illegal under California law.
  • An example: in 2015, LinkedIn was involved in a lawsuit, in which one of the main issues was the website asking new users for access to their email contacts, which the site would then use to spam the usersâ€™ contacts with invitations claiming to be from the user.
    • The provided screenshot shows LinkedIn's "Add to Network" page, in which clicking the Add button would cause the aforementioned friend spamming to occur.
  • To avoid friend spamming, when emailing a user's contacts, never sign the email with the user's own name.
